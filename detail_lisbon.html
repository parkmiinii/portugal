  1. Belém Tower

    Belém Tower is a historic landmark in Lisbon, Portugal, built between 1514-1520 by Manuel I and designated a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1983. It served as a fortress, lighthouse, and customs house, symbolizing Portugal's Age of Exploration. The tower, made of white marble, features Manueline-style stone carvings, including maritime motifs and a striking rhinoceros sculpture. From the top platform, visitors can enjoy stunning views of the Tagus River, Jerónimos Monastery, and the April 25 Bridge. Due to strong winds at the top, visitors should dress accordingly for protection and warmth.

    10:00-17:30(Admission ends at 17:00) Recommended sightseeing time:1-2 hours Av. Brasilia, 1400-038 Lisbon

  2. Jerónimos Monastery

    The Jerónimos Monastery, located in Lisbon’s Belém district, is a grand architectural masterpiece and a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1983. Built in 1501 by King Manuel I to honor Vasco da Gama’s voyage, it took nearly 100 years to complete. The monastery features intricate Manueline-style carvings, a magnificent south gate, and a vast Gothic nave with a high vaulted ceiling. Inside, Da Gama’s tomb and the poet Camões’ sarcophagus highlight Portugal’s maritime and literary legacy. The cloister offers stunning views of the courtyard, showcasing the elegance of Portuguese architecture.

    9:30-18:00(Admission ends at 17:30) Recommended sightseeing time:1-1.5 hours Praca do Imperio Belém, Lisbon 1400-206 Portugal

  3. São Jorge Castle

    São Jorge Castle is one of the oldest buildings in Lisbon and is the main historical monument and tourist attraction in Lisbon. The castle was built on the high point of the Alfama region and was the fortress when the Moors occupied Lisbon in the Middle Ages. Because it is located on a commanding hillside, it can be seen from almost every corner of Lisbon. The castle originally belonged to the earlier Visigoths. After the Moors invaded, it underwent a large-scale expansion. The 8-meter-thick city wall still makes people feel extraordinary. Here one can better appreciate the city's architectural art, whether it's the futuristic castle and drawbridge from Alfama to the city in the distance, or the open-air ruins of the Carmo church in the city.

    09:00-18:00(Admission ends at 17:30) Recommended sightseeing time:1-2 hours Address: Castle of S. Jorge, Rua de Santa Cruz, 1100-129 Lisbon

  4. Alfama

    Alfama Old Town is an ancient area of Lisbon with many historical monuments. It is located on a small hill in the Tejo Bay. The hillsides here are undulating, there are countless winding stone steps and alleys, and the old houses have strong colored outer walls, exuding a retro charm everywhere. There are many grocery stores on both sides of the streets in the old city that maintain the same appearance as they used to be, full of food and daily necessities. There are many bars and cafés, and it is the ideal place for Lisbon's nightlife.

    24hours Recommended sightseeing time:0.5-1 day Lisbon, Portugal

  5. Carmo Convent

    Carmo Monastery, located in Lisbon's Chiado district, was heavily damaged in the 1755 earthquake, leaving its Gothic church in ruins. Now an open-air museum, the Museu Arqueológico do Carmo displays religious sculptures, sarcophagi, and mummies. The monastery's historic cobbled streets and old facades reflect Lisbon's rich past. A hidden path next to the monastery leads to the top of the Santa Justa Lift, offering panoramic city views. From there, visitors can see landmarks like Rossio Square and São Jorge Castle Hill.

    10:00-18:00(Admission ends at 17:40) Recommended sightseeing time:1-2 hours Largo do Carmo 27, 1200-092 Lisboa, Portugal

  4. National Tile Museum

    The National Tile Museum in Lisbon showcases Portugal's rich tile history from the 15th century to today, featuring over 270 tile paintings. Its most notable exhibit is a 36-meter-long Lisbon tile painting. Housed in a 17th-century monastery, the museum explores the evolution of painted tiles, once symbols of wealth and status. It also displays international artistic tiles from France, Belgium, Germany, and the UK. Visitors can admire the collections, experience tile-making, and enjoy a meal in an 18th-century-style restaurant.

    10:00-18:00 Recommended sightseeing time:1-2 hours R. Me. Deus 4, 1900-312 Lisboa, Portugal
    see more

  5. Maritime Museum

    The Naval Museum, located in Lisbon's Belém district, was established between 1863-1963 by King Luís. Part of the Jerónimos Monastery, it showcases Portugal's rich maritime history dating back to the 15th century. The museum features historical paintings, archaeological finds, and an extensive collection of Portuguese ship models. A highlight is an 18th-century luxury yacht used by Queen Maria I and other Portuguese royals for two centuries. As one of Portugal’s most visited museums, it offers a fascinating glimpse into the nation's seafaring legacy.

    10:00-17:00 Recommended sightseeing time:1.5-2 hours Praça do Império, 1400-206 Lisbon

  1. Garden of the Calouste Gulbenkian Foundation

    The Garden of the Calouste Gulbenkian Foundation in Lisbon, Portugal, is a serene urban oasis surrounding the renowned Gulbenkian Museum. Designed in the 1960s by landscape architects Gonçalo Ribeiro Telles and António Viana Barreto, it features modernist garden aesthetics with lakes, winding paths, and diverse flora. The garden provides a peaceful retreat for visitors, offering spaces for relaxation, walking, and birdwatching. It also serves as a cultural hub, hosting open-air concerts, exhibitions, and artistic events. This green sanctuary is a must-visit for nature lovers and those seeking tranquility in the heart of the city.

    09:00-20:30 Recommended sightseeing time:1 hour Av. de Berna 45, 1050-078 Lisboa, Portugal

  2. Estufa Fria

    The Estufa Fria is a lush greenhouse garden located in Eduardo VII Park in Lisbon, Portugal. Opened in 1933, it consists of three sections: Estufa Fria (Cold Greenhouse), Estufa Quente (Hot Greenhouse), and Estufa Doce (Sweet Greenhouse), each housing exotic plants from around the world. The garden features waterfalls, small lakes, and winding paths, creating a peaceful retreat in the city. It is home to a diverse collection of tropical, subtropical, and Mediterranean flora. Estufa Fria is a perfect destination for nature lovers looking to explore a unique botanical paradise in Lisbon.

    10:00-19:00 Recommended sightseeing time:1 hour Parque Eduardo VII, 1070-051 Lisboa, Portugal
    see more

  3. Jardim Botanico Da Universidade De Lisboa

    The Jardim Botânico da Universidade de Lisboa is a historic botanical garden located in Lisbon, Portugal. Established in the 19th century, it was designed to support scientific research and education at the University of Lisbon. Covering about 4 hectares, the garden features a diverse collection of exotic plants from around the world, including rare tropical and subtropical species. It provides a tranquil escape with shaded pathways, palm trees, and ponds, making it a favorite spot for nature lovers. The garden also plays a key role in botanical conservation and environmental awareness programs.

    24hours Recommended sightseeing time:2-3 hours Rua Da Escola Politecnica 58, Lisbon 1250-102, Portugal

  4. Hell's Mouth

The waves of the Atlantic Ocean are even more frightening because of the cliffs and a deep hole in the cliffs. There is a small red-roofed house on the top. An old man is waiting here to welcome local and foreign tourists who come here from time to time. Standing on the shore, I saw the waves in the ocean rolling in, over the reefs near the shore, rolling out vortices one by one, and then furiously hitting the rocks and caves on the cliffs, making a deafening loud noise. Spectacular cliffs and huge waves and loud noises, hence the name.
